START HERE

You do not have to solve everything today.

Use this short checklist to organise the first steps.

1

Understand the immediate needs

Write down the diagnosis, current symptoms, medicines, mobility needs and upcoming appointments.

2

Identify your care team

Note the main doctor, nurse, medical social worker, therapist and family members involved.

3

Ask about support early

Discuss home care, respite, equipment, training, financial assistance and caregiver support groups.

4

Plan for yourself too

Choose one person you can contact, one regular break and one healthcare appointment you will not postpone.

A useful first question

“Who can help me coordinate the care plan after discharge?”

NAVIGATING CARE

Finding your way through care and support

Understand home care, centre-based care, respite, rehabilitation, nursing homes and hospice services.

Open guide

Start with the need

  • Medical or nursing care at home: ask the care team about home medical, home nursing or home palliative services.
  • Rehabilitation: ask about home therapy, day rehabilitation or outpatient therapy.
  • Supervision during the day: consider day care or dementia day care where suitable.
  • A short caregiver break: ask about respite options.
  • Long-term residential care: discuss nursing-home suitability with a medical social worker or care coordinator.

PLANNING AHEAD

ACP, LPA, Will and financial planning

Understand what each tool does, when it applies and how they work together.

Open guide
ACPRecords values and care preferences through conversations. It guides future healthcare discussions.
LPALets a person appoint trusted donee(s) to make decisions if mental capacity is lost.
WillStates how assets should be distributed after death and names an executor.

Conversation starters

  • “What matters most to you if your health worsens?”
  • “Who would you trust to speak for you?”
  • “Where are your important documents kept?”

Legal and financial information is general. Obtain professional advice for individual circumstances.

CARING AT HOME

Home safety, equipment and practical skills

Reduce falls, choose equipment carefully and protect both caregiver and care recipient.

Open guide

Room-by-room safety

  • Bathroom: non-slip surfaces, secure grab bars, adequate lighting and a suitable shower chair.
  • Bedroom: clear walking routes, reachable light switch, suitable bed height and stable bedside support.
  • Living areas: remove loose rugs and trailing cables; keep frequently used items within easy reach.

Before buying equipment

Ask an occupational therapist, physiotherapist or trained professional to assess fit, transfer technique and home layout. A wheelchair, commode or hospital bed that is poorly matched can create new risks.

Do not lift alone when the person's weight, balance or behaviour makes the transfer unsafe.

LOOKING AFTER YOURSELF

Caregiver burnout and coping strategies

Recognise strain early, reduce isolation and make support part of the care plan.

Open guide

Common signs of overload

  • Persistent exhaustion or poor sleep
  • Feeling irritable, numb, trapped or hopeless
  • Withdrawing from others
  • Missing your own medicines or appointments
  • Increasing conflict, unsafe handling or thoughts of harming yourself or someone else

A realistic coping plan

  1. Name one task another person can take over.
  2. Schedule a specific break rather than waiting for spare time.
  3. Tell a healthcare professional how caregiving is affecting you.
  4. Join a support group or speak with someone who understands caregiving.

Seek urgent help if you may harm yourself or another person, or if caregiving has become unsafe.

PALLIATIVE & HOSPICE CARE

Understanding comfort-focused care

Palliative care can be provided alongside treatment. Hospice services support people with serious, life-limiting illness and their families.

Open guide

Palliative care

Focuses on quality of life and relief of physical, psychological, social and spiritual distress. It is not limited to the final days.

Hospice services

May be delivered at home, in day programmes, inpatient hospices, community hospitals or through consultative support, depending on needs and eligibility.

Questions to ask

  • What symptoms can the team help manage?
  • Who should we call after office hours?
  • Can care be provided at home?
  • What support is available for the caregiver?

FINAL DAYS

Signs, comfort and communication

Know what changes may occur and how to focus on comfort while staying connected to the care team.

Open guide

Possible changes

People may sleep more, eat and drink less, become weaker, pass less urine, become confused or restless, and develop changes in breathing or skin temperature. Not every person experiences every sign.

Comfort measures

  • Offer food and fluids gently; do not force intake.
  • Provide mouth care and reposition for comfort as advised.
  • Keep the environment calm and speak normally, even if the person does not respond.
  • Use prescribed medicines exactly as instructed.
Call the care team for uncontrolled pain, severe breathlessness, distress, bleeding, a fall, medicine concerns or any change you are unsure about.

GRIEF SUPPORT

Before and after a loss

Grief can begin before death and may affect emotions, thoughts, sleep, appetite and relationships.

Open guide

There is no single correct timeline

People may feel sadness, relief, guilt, anger, numbness or several emotions at once. Support may include trusted people, faith communities, peer groups, counselling or bereavement services.

Seek additional help when

  • distress remains overwhelming or daily functioning is severely affected;
  • you are using alcohol, medicines or other substances to cope;
  • you have persistent thoughts of death or self-harm.

COMMUNICATION

Speaking with doctors, family and care recipients

Use clear questions, agree on responsibilities and make room for the care recipient's preferences.

Open guide

At a medical appointment

  • “What should we expect next?”
  • “What changes require urgent medical attention?”
  • “Which medicines are essential, and what side effects should we watch for?”
  • “Who can coordinate care after discharge?”

With family

Replace “I need more help” with a concrete request: “Can you take Mum to therapy every second Tuesday?” Record agreed tasks and review them regularly.

CHILDREN WITH SPECIAL NEEDS

Development, daily care and transitions

Track strengths and needs, work with professionals and plan ahead for school and adulthood.

Open guide

Practical caregiving principles

  • Use predictable routines and visual supports where helpful.
  • Break tasks into smaller steps and reinforce effort.
  • Observe triggers, communication needs and calming strategies.
  • Share the same strategies across home, school and therapy teams.

Planning transitions

Begin discussions early about early intervention, school pathways, therapy, transport, financial planning, post-school options, employment and long-term care.
