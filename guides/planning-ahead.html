Planning Ahead: ACP, LPA and Wills

Understand how Advance Care Planning, a Lasting Power of Attorney and a Will work together.

12 min read

Before you begin

Use this guide as a starting point. Individual needs differ, and service eligibility or legal requirements may change.

Why planning ahead matters

Planning ahead reduces uncertainty during illness or crisis. It gives families clearer guidance, makes decision-making less stressful and helps care teams understand what matters to the person receiving care.

Planning should happen while the person can participate meaningfully. It is not only for older adults or people who are already very unwell.

Advance Care Planning

Advance Care Planning, or ACP, is a process of discussing values, priorities and preferences for future healthcare. It can include what quality of life means to the person, who they trust to speak for them and what forms of treatment they may or may not want in certain situations.

ACP is not the same as refusing treatment. It helps future decisions reflect the person's values, even when circumstances change.

Lasting Power of Attorney

A Lasting Power of Attorney, or LPA, is a legal document that allows a person to appoint one or more donees to make personal welfare and/or property and affairs decisions if they lose mental capacity.

Choose donees carefully. Discuss expectations in advance and ensure they understand the person's values and practical circumstances.

Making a Will

A Will states how a person's estate should be distributed after death and appoints an executor to carry out those instructions.

Review the Will after major life changes such as marriage, divorce, the birth of a child, a significant change in assets or the death of an intended beneficiary or executor.

Conversation starters

Try: “I have been thinking about how we can make future decisions easier for everyone.”

Ask: “What matters most to you if your health worsens?” and “Who would you trust to speak on your behalf?”

Practical checklist

Locate identification, insurance, CPF, banking and property documents.

Record where the ACP, LPA and Will are kept and who has access.

Review documents and preferences periodically, especially after major health or family changes.
