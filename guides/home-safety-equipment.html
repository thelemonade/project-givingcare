Home care

Home Safety and Care Equipment

Reduce falls, choose equipment carefully and make the home easier to navigate.

11 min read

Before you begin

Use this guide as a starting point. Individual needs differ, and service eligibility or legal requirements may change.

Start with the person's routine

Observe how the person moves from bed to toilet, bathes, dresses, eats and enters or leaves the home. The most useful modifications are those that address real daily difficulties.

Ask an occupational therapist or physiotherapist for assessment when mobility, balance, cognition or transfers are complex.

Bathroom safety

Use suitable non-slip surfaces, secure professionally fitted grab bars, adequate lighting and a stable shower chair if recommended.

Avoid relying on towel rails or suction grab bars for body weight unless they are specifically designed and installed for that purpose.

Bedroom and living areas

Clear walking routes, remove loose rugs and trailing cables, keep a light switch within reach and ensure commonly used items are easy to access.

The bed and chair should allow safer transfers. A height that is too low or too high can increase falls and caregiver strain.

Choosing equipment

Before buying a wheelchair, commode, hospital bed or transfer device, check the user's measurements, posture, strength, environment and caregiver capacity.

Rental may be useful for short-term or changing needs. Ask about maintenance, cleaning, replacement parts and training.

Protecting the caregiver

Do not lift alone when the person's weight, balance or behaviour makes the transfer unsafe.

Use transfer techniques taught by a trained professional and stop if pain, loss of balance or unexpected resistance occurs.
