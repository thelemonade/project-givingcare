Caring for Children with Special Needs

Support development, daily routines, transitions and caregiver wellbeing.

13 min read

Before you begin

Use this guide as a starting point. Individual needs differ, and service eligibility or legal requirements may change.

Understand the individual child

Children with the same diagnosis can have very different strengths, communication styles, sensory preferences and support needs.

Track what helps the child participate, learn, communicate and recover from stress. Share useful observations with the school and therapy team.

Daily routines

Use predictable sequences, visual schedules and simple step-by-step instructions where helpful.

Prepare for transitions with warnings, visual cues and limited choices. Reinforce effort and participation rather than expecting immediate independence.

Sensory needs

Notice whether the child seeks or avoids movement, noise, touch, light, smell or certain textures.

Create a calm space and use sensory activities recommended by relevant professionals. Avoid unsafe materials, choking hazards and unmonitored water play.

Working with professionals

Ask what goals are being targeted, how progress is measured and what can be practised safely at home.

Seek clarification when recommendations are difficult to carry out. A plan that fits family routines is more likely to be sustained.

Planning transitions

Discuss early intervention, school pathways, therapy, transport, financial planning, post-school options, employment and long-term care well before the transition date.

Include the young person's preferences and strengths wherever possible.

Caregiver wellbeing

Caregivers may experience chronic stress, isolation and uncertainty about the future. Peer networks, respite, counselling and practical help can reduce the burden.

Ask directly about caregiver support rather than waiting for services to raise it.
