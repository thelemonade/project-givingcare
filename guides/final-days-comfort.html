End-of-life

Comfort During the Final Days

Recognise common changes, focus on comfort and know when to call the care team.

10 min read

Before you begin

Use this guide as a starting point. Individual needs differ, and service eligibility or legal requirements may change.

Possible changes

A person may sleep more, eat and drink less, become weaker, pass less urine, become confused or restless, and develop changes in breathing, circulation or skin temperature.

These signs can occur for different reasons. Contact the care team if you are uncertain or if changes are sudden or distressing.

Food and fluids

Offer small amounts gently when the person is awake and interested. Do not force food or fluids when swallowing is difficult or the person declines.

Mouth care, lip moisturiser and small sips when safe may provide more comfort than trying to meet usual intake.

Breathing and positioning

Repositioning, a calm environment, prescribed medicines and guidance from the care team may help with breathlessness or noisy breathing.

Speak normally and reassuringly. Hearing may remain even when the person does not respond.

When to call for help

Call the care team for uncontrolled pain, severe breathlessness, repeated vomiting, distress, bleeding, a fall, medicine concerns, new seizures or any change you do not know how to manage.

Follow the emergency plan provided by the treating or hospice team.
