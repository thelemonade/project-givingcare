Caregiver Burnout: Recognise It Early

Identify warning signs, reduce overload and make support part of the care plan.

10 min read

Before you begin

Use this guide as a starting point. Individual needs differ, and service eligibility or legal requirements may change.

What caregiver burnout can look like

Burnout may appear as persistent exhaustion, poor sleep, irritability, numbness, withdrawal, guilt, hopelessness or loss of interest in activities that previously mattered.

Physical signs can include headaches, muscle tension, changes in appetite and worsening of the caregiver's own health conditions.

Why it happens

Burnout is often driven by prolonged responsibility, uncertainty, disrupted sleep, financial pressure, family conflict, limited respite and the feeling that no one else fully understands the situation.

It is not a personal failure. It is a sign that the current care arrangement may be unsustainable.

A realistic action plan

List every recurring task. Mark what only you can do, what another family member can do, and what a service or professional could take over.

Make requests concrete: “Can you stay with Dad every Wednesday from 2 to 5 pm?” is easier to act on than “I need more help.”

Schedule breaks before reaching crisis point. Respite is part of safe care, not an optional reward.

Protecting your own health

Keep essential medical appointments, continue prescribed medicines and tell your doctor how caregiving is affecting sleep, mood, pain and daily functioning.

Maintain one identity outside caregiving through work, exercise, faith, friendships, hobbies or study.

When to seek urgent help

Seek immediate help if you may harm yourself or another person, if there is escalating violence or if caregiving tasks have become unsafe.

In a medical emergency, call 995. For immediate danger, call 999.
